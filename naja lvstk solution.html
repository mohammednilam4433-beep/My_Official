Livestock Care Guidance

Expert advice on animal health, nutrition, and management practices for Sri Lankan livestock farmers

Animal Health Issues

Common diseases and health problems affecting cattle, goats, and poultry. Symptoms include fever, loss of appetite, and abnormal behavior.

Feed Management

High cost and poor quality of animal feed leading to malnutrition and reduced productivity in livestock.

Breeding Problems

Low conception rates, difficult births, and poor offspring quality affecting livestock reproduction.

Shelter Management

Inadequate housing conditions leading to stress, disease spread, and reduced animal welfare.

Milk Production Issues

Low milk yield, poor milk quality, and mastitis problems in dairy animals affecting profitability.

Solutions for Animal Health Issues

Vaccination Program

Implement a regular vaccination schedule against common diseases like foot-and-mouth disease, hemorrhagic septicemia, and black quarter.

Benefits: Prevents disease outbreaks, reduces mortality, improves herd immunity

Natural Remedies

Use traditional herbal treatments like neem leaves for parasites, turmeric for wounds, and ginger for digestive issues.

Benefits: Low cost, easily available, minimal side effects

Regular Health Check-ups

Conduct weekly health inspections to detect problems early. Check temperature, appetite, behavior, and physical condition.

Benefits: Early disease detection, timely treatment, prevents spread

Quarantine New Animals

Isolate new animals for 2-3 weeks before introducing them to the main herd to prevent disease transmission.

Benefits: Prevents disease introduction, allows health monitoring, protects existing stock

Animal Health Management Guidelines

Vaccination Schedule

Follow this vaccination calendar for cattle:

  1. Foot-and-Mouth Disease: Every 6 months
  2. Hemorrhagic Septicemia: Annually before monsoon
  3. Black Quarter: Annually in endemic areas
  4. Brucellosis: For breeding animals only
  5. Anthrax: In high-risk areas annually

Disease Identification

Common symptoms to watch for:

  • Fever: Temperature above 102.5°F
  • Loss of appetite: Refusing feed for more than 24 hours
  • Abnormal discharge: From nose, eyes, or vagina
  • Lameness: Difficulty walking or standing
  • Behavior changes: Isolation, aggression, or depression

First Aid Kit

Maintain these essential items:

  • Thermometer
  • Antiseptic solution (iodine, betadine)
  • Bandages and cotton
  • Wound powder
  • Electrolyte powders
  • Common antibiotics (as prescribed by vet)

Solutions for Feed Management

Fodder Cultivation

Grow high-yield fodder crops like CO-4 grass, maize, and legumes on farm boundaries or dedicated plots.

Benefits: Reduces feed costs, ensures quality, available year-round

Silage Making

Preserve green fodder through anaerobic fermentation in pits or silos for use during dry seasons.

Benefits: Nutrient preservation, available offseason, reduces waste

Feed Formulation

Mix locally available ingredients to create balanced feed: 40% grains, 35% protein sources, 15% roughage, 10% minerals.

Benefits: Cost-effective, customized nutrition, better growth

Agricultural By-products

Utilize rice bran, wheat bran, oil cakes, and vegetable waste as supplementary feed ingredients.

Benefits: Low cost, reduces waste, nutritional value

Feed Management Guidelines

Daily Feeding Requirements

Recommended feeding for adult cattle (per day):

  • Green fodder: 25-30 kg
  • Dry fodder: 5-6 kg
  • Concentrate: 2-3 kg for maintenance, 1 kg extra per 2.5 liters milk
  • Mineral mixture: 50-60 grams
  • Salt: 25-30 grams
  • Water: 40-50 liters

Silage Preparation Steps

How to make quality silage:

  1. Harvest fodder at proper stage (maize at dough stage)
  2. Chop into 1-2 inch pieces
  3. Fill silo pit in layers, compacting well
  4. Cover with plastic sheet and soil
  5. Seal completely to exclude air
  6. Open after 45-60 days for feeding

Feed Storage Practices

Proper storage guidelines:

  • Store feed in clean, dry, and ventilated areas
  • Keep away from moisture and direct sunlight
  • Use raised platforms to prevent rodent access
  • Practice first-in-first-out rotation
  • Check regularly for mold or pests

Solutions for Breeding Problems

Artificial Insemination

Use AI with quality semen from proven bulls to improve genetic quality and conception rates.

Benefits: Better genetics, disease prevention, cost-effective

Heat Detection

Train staff to identify heat signs accurately: restlessness, mucus discharge, mounting other animals.

Benefits: Proper timing, higher conception, efficient breeding

Nutrition Management

Provide balanced nutrition before and after breeding, including mineral supplements and extra energy.

Benefits: Better body condition, improved fertility, healthy pregnancy

Breeding Records

Maintain detailed records of heat dates, breeding dates, expected calving, and offspring performance.

Benefits: Better planning, genetic tracking, performance analysis

Breeding Management Guidelines

Heat Detection Protocol

Steps for effective heat detection:

  1. Observe animals 3 times daily (morning, noon, evening)
  2. Look for primary signs: standing to be mounted, clear mucus discharge
  3. Note secondary signs: restlessness, bellowing, swollen vulva
  4. Use heat detection aids if available
  5. Breed 12 hours after first standing heat

Pregnancy Care

Management during pregnancy:

  • Provide extra nutrition in last 3 months
  • Avoid stressful handling
  • Vaccinate as per schedule
  • Prepare calving area 2 weeks before due date
  • Watch for calving signs: udder development, relaxation of pelvic ligaments

Newborn Care

Essential care for newborn calves:

  • Clear mucus from nose and mouth immediately
  • Ensure colostrum feeding within 2 hours
  • Dip navel in iodine solution
  • Keep in clean, dry, and warm environment
  • Monitor for normal standing and suckling

Solutions for Shelter Management

Proper Ventilation

Design shelters with adequate airflow to prevent respiratory issues and reduce heat stress.

Benefits: Better air quality, reduced disease risk, improved comfort

Drainage System

Install proper drainage to keep animal housing dry and prevent foot problems and parasite infestation.

Benefits: Dry flooring, reduced infections, better hygiene

Space Management

Provide adequate space per animal to prevent overcrowding and reduce stress and aggression.

Benefits: Reduced injuries, better welfare, improved productivity

Shelter Management Guidelines

Space Requirements

Minimum space per animal:

  • Adult cattle: 40-50 sq ft per animal
  • Calves: 20-25 sq ft per animal
  • Goats/Sheep: 15-20 sq ft per animal
  • Poultry: 1-2 sq ft per bird

Shelter Design

Key design considerations:

  • East-west orientation for proper sunlight
  • Height of 8-10 feet for proper ventilation
  • Concrete flooring with proper slope for drainage
  • Separate areas for feeding, resting, and calving

Solutions for Milk Production Issues

Proper Milking Hygiene

Implement strict milking hygiene practices including udder washing, teat dipping, and clean equipment.

Benefits: Reduces mastitis, improves milk quality, longer shelf life

Balanced Nutrition

Provide balanced feed with adequate energy, protein, minerals, and vitamins for optimal milk production.

Benefits: Higher milk yield, better milk quality, improved animal health

Regular Milking Schedule

Maintain consistent milking times and intervals to establish a regular milk let-down reflex.

Benefits: Consistent production, reduced stress, better milk quality

Milk Production Guidelines

Milking Procedure

Steps for proper milking:

  1. Wash udder with clean water and dry with separate towel
  2. Check for signs of mastitis (swelling, pain, abnormal milk)
  3. Use clean, sanitized equipment
  4. Milk completely and quickly
  5. Dip teats in antiseptic solution after milking

Milk Handling

Proper milk handling practices:

  • Filter milk immediately after milking
  • Cool to below 4°C within 2 hours
  • Store in clean, food-grade containers
  • Transport in insulated containers
